Morocco: The Waiting Room for Europe's Backend

January 10, 2026

Walk into any coworking space in Casablanca, from Technopark to the chic cafes of Gauthier, and you will hear the hum of productivity. But if you listen closely to the keystrokes and the hushed conversations over half-caf lattes, you aren't hearing the sound of a nation building its future. You are hearing the sound of a departure lounge.

Our brightest minds, the ones who understand memory management in C and the intricacies of distributed systems, are not building solutions for Morocco. They are treating this country like a staging environment before deploying to production in Germany, France, or Canada.

Why? Is it just the Euros? Is it just the healthcare?

No. That’s the lazy answer. The uncomfortable truth is that the Moroccan software industry is functionally designed to be a digital sweatshop. We have built an ecosystem that strips engineers of their agency, treats code as a commodity, and actively discourages the one thing that keeps a hacker alive: Ownership.

The Curse of the "Body Shop" (ESN)

Let's strip away the corporate euphemisms. In Morocco, we don't have many "Tech Companies." We have "ESNs" (Entreprises de Services du Numérique). In English, we call them Body Shops.

Here is their business model:

  1. Hire a junior dev fresh out of school for 8,000 DH/month.
  2. Rent them out to a French bank or insurance giant for €400/day.
  3. Keep the difference.
  4. Repeat until the developer burns out or leaves.

This is not engineering; this is feudalism with Jira tickets. The developer is not building a product; they are "human resources" sold by the kilo. You are not solving a problem; you are patching a legacy Java 6 monolith for a client who sees you as a cost center, not a creator.

When you work in this model, you have zero user freedom. You have zero creative control. You are a digital bricklayer building someone else's castle. Is it any wonder that our best engineers look at this bleak horizon of endless maintenance tickets and think, "If I’m going to be a cog in the machine, I might as well be a cog in Berlin earning €70k"?

Digital Colonialism 2.0

We need to talk about the geopolitical architecture of our code. The "Offshore" model is simply colonialism rebranded for the 21st century. Instead of extracting phosphates or silver, the Global North now extracts cognitive labor.

They send us the grunt work—the unit tests, the documentation, the maintenance of dying codebases. They keep the architecture, the decision-making, and the Intellectual Property (IP) in Paris or Silicon Valley. We get the wages; they get the wealth.

This creates a perverse incentive structure in our education system. We aren't teaching students to solve Moroccan problems—water scarcity, agricultural logistics, rural education. We are training them to be compatible with French corporate stacks. We teach React and Spring Boot not because they are the best tools for us, but because that’s what the market in Paris demands.

We are optimizing our youth for export.

The "Resume-Driven Development" Trap

Because everyone knows they are leaving, nobody invests in the local ecosystem. Why build a robust open-source library for Moroccan e-government integration if you're going to be in Munich in six months?

This leads to Resume-Driven Development (RDD). Developers choose technologies based on what looks good on a visa application, not what is good for the user or the project.

// The Moroccan Dev's Dilemma

if (technology === "Simple & Effective") {
career_prospects--; // "Nobody in Germany hires for PHP/Laravel anymore"
} else if (technology === "Complex & Trendy") {
career_prospects++; // "Kubernetes microservices! You're hired!"
}

The result? We over-engineer everything. We build simple websites using complex microservices architectures because the developer needs to prove they know Kubernetes to get that job at Zalando. The local user suffers with a slow, bloated app, but the developer gets their visa. It’s a tragedy of the commons where the "commons" is our national digital infrastructure.

The Illusion of "Tech Sovereignty"

True sovereignty isn't just about having a flag; it's about owning your data and your code. Right now, Morocco is drifting towards being a client state of Big Tech. We use Microsoft for our government email, Oracle for our databases, and AWS for our hosting.

This is dangerous. When you build your house on someone else's land, you don't own the house. You are a tenant. If Microsoft decides to change their licensing terms (as they frequently do), we pay. If Google changes their API, our businesses break.

The only way out is Free and Open Source Software (FOSS). Not as a hobby, but as a strategic imperative.

The Solution: Build Here, Own Here

We cannot blame an engineer for wanting a better life. But we can challenge the definition of a "better life." Is it better to be a highly paid employee in a cold country, or a founder and builder in your own?

To the developers reading this: Stop waiting.

Stop treating your current location as a waiting room. Start treating it as a laboratory. The problems in Morocco are hard. Fixing the supply chain for small farmers in the Atlas Mountains is a harder distributed systems problem than optimizing ad clicks for a Berlin startup. And it matters more.

1. Reject the Body Shop

If you have the skills, refuse to work for companies that rent you out. Freelance directly. Build products. Form cooperatives. Join startups that actually make something. If the local market doesn't value you, work remotely for global clients who respect output over "butt-in-seat" time, but keep your spending and your tax money here.

2. Embrace Radical Simplicity

Stop importing complexity. Our users often have older phones and expensive 4G data. Building a 5MB JavaScript bundle for a news site isn't "modern"; it's disrespectful to the user. Write efficient, clean code. HTML and CSS are not beneath you; they are the bedrock of a free web.

3. Contribute to Open Source (Strategically)

Don't just fix typos in Facebook's React documentation. Build tools that we need. Where is the open-source library for Darija NLP? Where is the public API for ONCF train schedules (that doesn't require scraping)? Where is the mapping data for our rural roads?

"The power of the Open Source philosophy is that it allows us to stop being consumers of technology and start being creators of culture."

Conclusion: The Visa is Not the Victory

Getting a Blue Card to Germany is an achievement, yes. It validates your skills. But it is not the victory. The victory is when a Moroccan engineer can stay in Khenichet or Agadir, push code to a repository they own, solve a problem for their neighbor, and make a global living doing it.

The internet promised to flatten the world. Instead, corporate feudalism and the consultancy model have turned it into a hierarchy where we are at the bottom.

It’s time to stop coding for the exit. It’s time to code for the revolution. It’s time to build a digital ecosystem that is so vibrant, so sovereign, and so exciting that the recruiters in Berlin have to start calling us to ask how we did it.

Now, go check your git status. Are you committing to your own future, or just to someone else's repository?
